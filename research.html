Research

From protein to patterned device

Our work runs from materials science — making natural biopolymers respond to light — through microfabrication, mechanical design, and finally functional devices such as biosensors.

Photoreactive biomaterials

Making natural polymers behave like photoresists without giving up what makes them biomaterials.

Photofibroin and photosericin

Methacrylated silk fibroin (photofibroin, 2013) and methacrylated silk sericin (photosericin, 2015) are protein resists that crosslink under light. They show enhanced stability in aqueous environments, preserve microscale architecture, and are mechanically tunable. The fabrication route is entirely green and protein-based. Photosericin has been licensed to Advanced BioMatrix.

Elastomeric photofibroin

Doping photofibroin with <5% UV-curable siloxane yields extraordinary strength and versatility while retaining cytocompatibility and biodegradation over roughly a month. These substrates support silver circuit patterning and are compatible with traditional microfabrication.

Gelatin composites

Gelatin resembles native extracellular matrix and is inexpensive and versatile, but it is unstable at biological temperature and mechanically weak. Our work involves designing various forms of gelatin-based composites. We have reported enzymatic crosslinking with microbial transglutaminase (MxGel), doping with PEDOT:PSS for electroconductive films (CMxGel), and polydopamine (PDA) coatings to improve strength and elasticity. Combined with photofibroin, we can form versatile substrates for functional electronics and cellular engineering.

Patterning and fabrication

Spatial control across five orders of magnitude, using intrinsic material properties and adding new function.

Nanoscale patterning

Electron beam lithography forms protein nanowires and nanostructures, made conductive with PEDOT:PSS.

2D formats

Flexible micropatterned sheets and shape-defined particles produced through photolithographic patterning of protein resists.

3D printing

Photofibroin and its composites can be 3D printed using DLP, producing centimeter-scale structures. Conducting hydrogels can combine photofibroin with PEDOT:PSS.

Kirigami mechanics

Cuts, not chemistry, as the design variable for compliance at intrinsically dynamic biointerfaces.

Kirigami-inspired films

Computational design of cuts

Finite element modeling visualizes stress concentration near cut tips under increasing load for slit and branched (Y-shaped) geometries, letting us design cuts or material openings to target specific levels of stretch and modes of bending before anything is fabricated.

Flexible bioelectronics

Water-based, room-temperature, bench-top processes yielding fully organic circuits and transient devices.

Conductive silk inks

Photosericin combined with PEDOT:PSS gives flexible, fully organic circuits printed at room temperature from water. Our work has created dual-function inks incorporating iron oxide nanoparticles (PSION) to form circuits that can act as magnetic connect/disconnect nodes.

Biosensors

We have developed various selective and non-selective electrochemical biosensors built from conductive ink plus enzymes, antibodies etc. for detection of analytes in blood, serum, and other biofluids.

Temperature sensing and energy storage

Skin-mounted flexible temperature sensors weighing about 3.7 mg/cm², alongside biopolymer micro-supercapacitors roughly 27 times lighter than office paper. Both degrade in an enzymatic (protease) environment over about 21 days while remaining stable in plain aqueous conditions — degradation on demand rather than by accident.

Cellular engineering

Cell micropatterning

Photofibroin micropatterns direct selective osteoblast adhesion to 10 µm lines and support L929 fibroblast culture. The materials and patterns degrade enzymatically in a controllable way.

Electrical stimulation

Covalently attached organic bioink patterns align cells and deliver stimulation. Over two weeks, stimulation increases myotube width and length — indicators of cell fusion and development.

Biosecurity and authentication

Protein self-assembly as a stochastic image generator

Silk species can dictate architectural variation in diffusion-limited aggregation: Bombyx mori gives dendritic architectures, Antheraea mylitta orthogonal ones, and Antheraea assamensis spherulites. Binarized nanoscale images of this self-assembly can be used to generate cryptographic keys for enrollment, storage, and later validation — randomness in nature turned into a physically unclonable function.

Printed security labels

Maskless, rapid digital printing places a high-contrast photosericin bioink pattern on a photofibroin sheet, with fiduciary markers accounting for distortion, lighting, and camera resolution. The flexible sheets adhere to non-planar geometry and degrade proteolytically. Applications include tamper-evident labels, secure QR codes, anti-counterfeiting for pharmaceuticals, and product tracing.
