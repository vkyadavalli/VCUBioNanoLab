Department of Chemical and Life Science Engineering

Light-actuated protein biomaterials for devices at the biointerface

We design photoreactive silk fibroin and sericin biomaterials that can be patterned with light, then build them into flexible, degradable sensors, circuits, and cell-instructive substrates.

Explore our research Join us

What we do

Silk proteins are abundant, inexpensive, optically transparent, mechanically tunable, and degradable. By making them photocrosslinkable, we can integrate them with microfabrication — photolithography, electron beam lithography, and digital light 3D printing — using water as a solvent at room temperature.

Materials

Photoreactive biopolymers

Photofibroin, photosericin, elastomeric photofibroin, and photogelatin composites — protein resists that pattern like a photoresist but behave like a biomaterial.

Fabrication

Micro- and nanopatterning

Photolithography, e-beam lithography, maskless and DLP 3D printing, and kirigami-inspired cut geometries that tune stretch, bending, and transport.

Devices

Flexible bioelectronics

Enzymatic and impedimetric biosensors, temperature sensors, micro-supercapacitors, and fully organic conductive circuits — functional, flexible, and fully degradable.

Cells

Cellular engineering

Micropatterned substrates that direct cell adhesion and alignment, with electrical stimulation driving myotube maturation over multi-week culture.

Security

Biosecurity and authentication

Protein self-assembly as a stochastic image generator for physically unclonable functions, secure labels, and anti-counterfeiting.

Modeling

Computational design

Finite element modeling of cut geometries to predict stress concentration and engineer mechanical response before fabrication.

Guiding idea

Nature-inspired strategies using silk proteins can lead to sustainable technologies in bioelectronics and biodevices. Light provides added versatility in design and fabrication, and the benign, water-based process allows immobilization of antibodies, enzymes, and DNA. The resulting devices are transient — their lifespan can be tuned, and they degrade enzymatically while remaining stable in aqueous environments.
